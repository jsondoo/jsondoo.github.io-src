Being a Teaching Assistant

June 5th, 2017

So after being a teaching assistant for 8 months, I thought I would write about my experience.

From Sept-Dec 2016, I was a teaching assistant for APSC 160, an introductory programming course for engineering students in C. You mainly learn about the basics such as if/else, loops, and functions, and an additional hardware component at the end.

There was about four lecture sections, taught by Celina Berg, Ed Knorr, and Jonatan Schroeder. We had weekly meetings, where instructors would give us an update on what the upcoming week's lab is about, collect lab attendance sheets, and have a general discussion about how everything is going.

Lab sections were kind of like exams. Students were not allowed to talk to their friends, use their phones, or access the internet for help. My role as a TA was to pass out the lab worksheet, answer any general questions, and make sure that nobody was cheating. I am not sure why it is so strict but it's been this way for years. We were also not allowed to help them with the lab until it was over, so the other TA and I usually held short office hour sessions to explain concepts to struggling students.

Marking labs was a LOT of work. Usually it would take me two hours to mark a section of 20-30 students. First, you download a folder from the Connect Gradebook which contains source code files for each student in the section. Then, you compile the code, run it, test it with different inputs, make sure they had sufficient comments, and use a rubric on Connect to assign them a mark.

Sounds straight forward but half of the time the code did not even compile. And if it did there were infinite loops or buggy outputs. This was a pain, because then you have to carefully examine their code, decide on how to give part marks, and take extra time to leave feedback. So depending on the quality of the code, marking a single student could take anywhere from 2 minutes to 15 minutes. Not to mention, dealing with emails and updating marks when students switch lab sections drained even more time. I did improve my efficiency and debugging skills over time though.

Marking midterms and finals was a bit better. You get assigned a question to mark, and you just go through a pile of exams, give a mark, and pass it on. For coding questions, there's always many ways to implement it, so you really had to carefully step through the code, and reach a consensus on giving part marks. It's fun because you get to see all kinds of solutions - unreadable solutions, clever solutions, or solutions that make you wonder what the hell they were thinking.

Oh, and free food. For finals marking, we started off our morning with bagels, yogurt, bananas, and coffee. Later, we ordered some indian food - trays of rice, curry, and chicken wings, and bottles of pop. It was pretty much a full course meal.

Overall, being a teaching assistant was an amazing experience. It was a great opportunity for me to interact with professors, and really get to know them - they are all wonderful people that I still keep in touch with. It was lot of fun talking to students, and engaging with them throughout the course. Engineering is tough, and it's rewarding to help struggling students, give them advice on how to succeed not only in the course, but in academics in general, and watch them improve their programming skills. I would definitely do it again :)
